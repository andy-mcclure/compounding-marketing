**1. Names the problem precisely.**
Not vendor incompetence. Misownership. The intelligence lives in the wrong place — and no amount of agency-switching fixes a structural problem.

**2. Establishes the category.**
The In-House Growth Machine as architecture. Compounding Marketing as discipline. Both named and defined for the first time.

**3. Functions as a positioning asset.**
Educates prospects before the first conversation. Clients who arrive having read the book arrive already converted to the ownership thesis.

---

## Core Arguments

*"Marketing isn't broken. It's just that nobody owns it."*

The agency model creates three compounding problems: loss of control, lack of continuity, inability to compound.

*"If you don't own the environment where your marketing lives, you don't own the intelligence it generates."*

The solution is owning the system — the accounts, data, AI workspaces, workflows. Not the agency relationship. The environment.

*"The architect doesn't pour concrete. The architect ensures the concrete gets poured in the right place at the right time for the right reason."*

The owner's role is not operator. It is owner. The distinction matters.

*"Tactics give you a spike that fades. Systems give you a slope that compounds."*

The alternative to reset is not better tactics. It is a different structure.

AI is a force multiplier — it amplifies whatever structure it operates inside. Inside a system the owner controls, AI compounds. Inside an agency's infrastructure, AI accelerates the owner's dependency.
